# What Doesn't Work: Anti-Pattern Examples

Concrete examples of writing that fails, and why. Use this as a diagnostic — if your draft resembles any of these, rewrite.

## How to use this file

Read this when:
- Your draft feels off but you can't say why
- You want to show a user concretely what to avoid
- You're reviewing existing copy that's underperforming
- You need to explain *why* a common approach fails

Each example below shows a failed pattern + diagnosis + the rewrite.

---

## Failed Hook #1: The "Are You Struggling" Opener

**The failed version:**
> "Are you struggling to grow your online business? Tired of feeling overwhelmed? In this video I'll share my proven 3-step framework..."

**Why it fails:**
- "Struggling" + "tired" + "overwhelmed" is generic pain that filters for nobody. Every viewer thinks "well, sort of" and scrolls.
- "Proven 3-step framework" is ad-speak. Brain pattern-matches to "low quality."
- Opens with "In this video I'll share" — the audio equivalent of a slow intro screen.

**The rewrite:**
> "Last Tuesday I refunded a client because I couldn't ship their report on time. That was the third one in two months. Here's the workflow I built that Friday that fixed it."

**What changed:**
- Specific moment ("last Tuesday"), specific stake (refund), specific number (third one)
- Personal story = trust signal
- Mid-thought open ("Last Tuesday I...") instead of self-narrating

## Failed Hook #2: The Generic Curiosity Bait

**The failed version:**
> "You won't BELIEVE what happened when I tried this one weird trick! Stay until the end to find out the secret nobody is talking about..."

**Why it fails:**
- "You won't believe" + "one weird trick" + "the secret nobody is talking about" — three dead patterns stacked.
- Audiences trained over a decade to recognize and dismiss these.
- Hides the value behind a wall — "stay until the end" is a guarantee viewers will scroll.

**The rewrite:**
> "I deleted 11 of my 12 SaaS subscriptions last month. My business doubled. Here's the math."

**What changed:**
- Specific count (11 of 12) instead of "weird trick"
- Counterintuitive result stated up front (no "stay till the end")
- Promise of evidence ("here's the math") — invites scrutiny instead of avoiding it

## Failed Hook #3: The Long Setup

**The failed version:**
> "Hey guys! So in today's video I want to talk about something I've been working on for the past few years that I think you're really going to love. It's been a real journey and I can't wait to share it with you..."

**Why it fails:**
- "Hey guys, so today" = the lowest-stakes audio signal possible
- 25 seconds before any value is delivered
- "Journey" is a content-marketing red flag — audiences expect vagueness
- Future tense ("you're going to love") = nothing has happened yet

**The rewrite:**
> "I just built a client reporting system in 90 minutes. Runs Friday at 5pm. Drafts Sunday morning. Cost: $0. Here's the build."

**What changed:**
- No greeting, no preamble, no "I want to talk about"
- Specific number (90 min), specific time (Friday 5pm), specific cost ($0)
- Present perfect ("I just built") = it actually exists
- "Here's the build" promises immediate delivery, not buildup

## Failed Body #1: All Tell, No Show

**The failed version:**
> "This workshop is amazing. You'll learn so much. It's truly transformative. Sarah from our last cohort had incredible results. You can't afford to miss this opportunity to level up your business."

**Why it fails:**
- "Amazing," "transformative," "incredible," "level up" — banned hype words, every single one
- "Sarah" is named but not specifically described — no result, no specifics
- "Can't afford to miss" is generic FOMO with no real urgency
- Zero specific information about what's actually inside

**The rewrite:**
> "Saturday at 10am, we build one specific workflow together — your choice from these four: client reporting, email triage, content scheduling, or proposal drafting. By Sunday, you have a working version running in your business. Sarah Kowalski, last cohort's bookkeeper, built her client reporting workflow in 87 minutes and saved 11 hours her first week."

**What changed:**
- Specific time (Saturday 10am), specific options (the four workflows)
- Specific outcome (working version by Sunday)
- Sarah is fully named with role, specific time (87 min), and specific result (11 hours)
- Zero banned hype words

## Failed Body #2: Multiple Topics, No Anchor

**The failed version:**
> "We'll cover hooks, headlines, frameworks, voice, formats, conversion psychology, and more. By the end you'll have a complete understanding of modern marketing across all platforms."

**Why it fails:**
- 7+ topics promised in one breath = the script will be shallow on each
- "Complete understanding of modern marketing" is a promise nobody can deliver in one video
- Audiences trust focus more than breadth — promising less convincingly beats promising everything

**The rewrite:**
> "We'll cover one thing: how to write the first 3 seconds of a Meta video ad so that 50% of viewers keep watching past the hook. That's it. By the end you'll have three hooks written for your own offer, ready to test."

**What changed:**
- One specific topic (first 3 seconds of Meta video)
- Specific metric (50% past hook)
- Specific deliverable (three hooks, ready to test)
- Focused promise sounds more credible than expansive promise

## Failed CTA #1: The Multi-CTA Pile-Up

**The failed version:**
> "If you liked this video, hit subscribe! Also follow me on Instagram for more tips. And check out my newsletter at the link in bio. And if you want to work with me, DM me 'WORK.' Let me know in the comments what you'd like to see next!"

**Why it fails:**
- Five separate CTAs in 15 seconds
- Each one cannibalizes the others
- Viewer's brain triages and does nothing
- "Let me know in the comments" alone often outperforms this entire pile

**The rewrite:**
> "If you want the workshop, link in description. Saturday, 10am, $17. That's the only thing I want you to do — click the link, save your seat. See you there."

**What changed:**
- One CTA, repeated with specifics
- Closes the loop ("that's the only thing")
- Specific destination, specific date, specific price
- No tangential asks

## Failed CTA #2: The Apologetic Pitch Transition

**The failed version:**
> "Okay, I know this is the salesy part and I really hate doing this, but if you're still here and you want more, I have a course that maybe could help. Sorry for the plug but..."

**Why it fails:**
- "Salesy part" — signals you think your offer is shameful
- "I really hate doing this" — signals you think the offer is bad
- "Maybe could help" — hedge words destroy conversion
- "Sorry for the plug" — apologizing for serving the audience
- All of this tells the viewer: this product probably isn't worth buying

**The rewrite:**
> "If what we covered today resonated, here's how to keep going. The AI Workflow Builder cohort opens Tuesday. We build four workflows together over four weeks. $497. Link below."

**What changed:**
- Confident transition ("here's how to keep going")
- Specific offer (4 workflows, 4 weeks)
- Specific price
- No apology, no hedging, no "sorry"

## Failed Webinar #1: 90% Teaching, 10% Pitch

**The failed version:**
- 0:00-1:20: Origin story
- 1:20-1:25: "Okay so the offer is the cohort, link below" [end]

**Why it fails:**
- All teaching, no time for the offer to land
- Viewers haven't been told what to do
- No stack, no value building, no anchoring
- No Q&A — the conversion event was skipped

**The rewrite (proper webinar pacing):**
- 0:00-0:15: Welcome + origin story
- 0:15-0:45: Three Secrets (each breaks a false belief, gives a tactic)
- 0:45-0:55: The Stack (5 components, named values)
- 0:55-1:00: Price reveal, guarantee, CTA
- 1:00-1:30+: Q&A (where most conversions happen)

## Failed Webinar #2: Surprise Offer at Minute 50

**The failed version:**
Spends 50 minutes teaching with no mention of an offer. At 50:00, suddenly: "Oh and by the way, I have a course you can buy."

**Why it fails:**
- Audience feels bait-and-switched
- No anchoring has happened — the price feels random
- No trial closes throughout — audience isn't in "yes" mode
- Buyers who wanted to know "what's next" weren't given the heads-up

**The rewrite (tease the offer early):**
- At minute 3: "By the end of this I'll also show you how to do this 10x faster — stay till the end."
- At minute 25: "This is the part of the program I'm offering at the end — the one we just covered is from week 2."
- At minute 45: "Okay, ready for what comes next? Let me show you the program."

The offer is expected, not a surprise.

## Failed Static Ad: The Brand-First Layout

**The failed version:**
```
[LARGE BRAND LOGO TOP CENTER]
[TAGLINE: "Empowering creators since 2020"]
[STOCK PHOTO of smiling people with laptops]
[SMALL TEXT: "Check out our new offering"]
[LEARN MORE button]
```

**Why it fails:**
- Logo is the focal point (you're not Nike — no brand equity to coast on)
- "Empowering creators" is generic and could apply to any company
- Stock photo of smiling people = ignored by trained eye
- "Check out our new offering" = zero specificity
- "Learn more" = the weakest CTA

**The rewrite:**
```
[TOP — large bold]
"I cancelled 3 SaaS tools last month."

[CENTER — annotated screenshot of a workflow]
[Arrow]: "This $40 workflow replaced $348/month"

[BOTTOM — bold]
"Get the template — free →"

[Tiny logo, bottom right]
```

**What changed:**
- Hook is the focal point, not the logo
- Specific numbers ($40, $348/month, 3 SaaS tools)
- Annotated screenshot = proof + interest
- Specific CTA ("Get the template — free")
- Logo demoted to where it belongs for direct-response

## Failed Email: The Corporate Newsletter

**The failed version:**
```
Subject: November Newsletter - Updates and Insights

Dear Valued Customer,

We hope this email finds you well. We have so many exciting updates to share with you this month! Our team has been working tirelessly to bring you new features and content...

[3 sections of generic updates]

[Footer with logo, address, social links, unsubscribe]
```

**Why it fails:**
- "November Newsletter" subject = predictable, ignored
- "Dear Valued Customer" = mass-broadcast signal
- "We hope this email finds you well" = filler, opt-out trigger
- "Exciting updates" + "working tirelessly" = corporate ad-speak
- Multiple sections = no clear single message
- HTML-heavy = looks like marketing, not personal

**The rewrite:**
```
Subject: the night I almost refunded a client

Hey,

Last Tuesday at 11pm I was 4 hours into building a client report. The kind I'd already promised would be delivered by 9am. I almost refunded the client just to get the sleep.

That Friday I built the workflow that handles it now. Takes 17 minutes.

If you want the template (with the Notion + ChatGPT setup), here it is: [LINK]

That's it. No pitch.

Mike

P.S. The reason I almost refunded wasn't the work. It was realizing I'd be doing the same thing next Tuesday. The template's free.
```

**What changed:**
- Lowercase subject feels personal, not broadcast
- "Hey" instead of "Dear Valued Customer"
- Specific story with specific times (Tuesday 11pm, 4 hours, 9am, 17 min)
- No HTML, no formatting tricks — plain text
- One link, one CTA
- P.S. delivers additional insight, not a pitch

## Patterns across all failed examples

When you look at what makes these examples fail, the same anti-patterns appear:

1. **Generic openers** ("Are you struggling," "Hey guys," "Dear Valued Customer")
2. **Banned hype words** (amazing, incredible, transformative, level up)
3. **Multiple competing CTAs** (subscribe + follow + newsletter + DM)
4. **Hedging language** (might, maybe, could possibly, sort of)
5. **Vague proof** (thousands, lots, many, incredible results)
6. **Apologetic pitches** (sorry for the plug, salesy part, hate to do this)
7. **Buried specifics** (waiting until "stay till the end" to deliver value)
8. **Brand-first instead of value-first** (logo as focal point, tagline before benefit)

If your draft contains any 3+ of these, it will underperform regardless of framework. Cut them.

## The diagnostic question

When reviewing any draft, ask:

> "If I were scrolling through a feed and saw this, would I stop? Would I read the second line? Would I take the action?"

If the honest answer to any of those is "no," the script needs revision before delivery. Don't ship to the user something you wouldn't act on yourself.
