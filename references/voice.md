# Frameworks Reference Library

Every high-converting script uses one of these structural frameworks. Pick one based on length and goal. The framework is the skeleton; the hook type is the face; the voice (in references/voice.md) is the skin.

## How to choose a framework

| Length / Goal | Use this framework |
|---|---|
| Short ad (15-60 sec, single goal) | Hook-Body-CTA |
| Mid ad with clear pain to amplify | PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve) |
| When the audience needs to see a transformation | BAB (Before-After-Bridge) |
| When you have strong proof to lean on | 4Ps (Promise-Picture-Proof-Push) |
| Emails and long-form content | AIDA (legacy, still useful) |
| Long video sales letter (3-15 min) | Extended VSL structure |
| Webinar / workshop (45-90 min) | Perfect Webinar (Origin → 3 Secrets → Stack → Close) |

## Framework 1: Hook-Body-CTA (The Universal Default)

The simplest, most reliable framework. Use this when in doubt. Three sections:

- **Hook (3-5 seconds):** Pick a hook type from `references/hooks.md`.
- **Body (15-45 seconds):** Deliver on the hook's promise. Show, don't tell. Include proof.
- **CTA (3-5 seconds):** One specific next step.

**Why it works:** Linear, minimal cognitive load, mirrors how the brain naturally processes information (orient → understand → decide). For first-time writers, this is the default. Hard to mess up.

**Three examples:**

**A — Curiosity hook + demonstration body + CTA:**
> "I cancelled 3 SaaS tools last month. [show 3 logos struck through]. Here's the $40 workflow I replaced them with. [screen record 20 seconds]. The template's free. Link below."

**B — Pain hook + story body + CTA:**
> "It's 11pm and you're still building the proposal. [cut to messy desk]. I lived in this for 18 months. Then I built this template. [show template]. Last week's proposal: 23 minutes. Comment 'PROPOSAL' and I'll send it."

**C — Stat hook + breakdown body + CTA:**
> "$11,847 from one workshop. Here's the 47-minute structure: [3 quick bullets]. Full breakdown in the free workshop Tuesday. Sign up free."

**Anti-patterns:**
- Hook + Body + Body + Body + maybe a CTA at the end. Multiple bodies dilute everything.
- Hook + skipping body straight to "buy now." Nothing earned the buy.

## Framework 2: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)

The classic direct-response framework. Identify the pain, magnify it, then resolve it. Works because it creates empathy before offering a solution.

- **Problem:** Name it specifically.
- **Agitate:** Make it worse. Surface the second-order consequences. ("And then...")
- **Solve:** Show the way out. Position your offer as the resolution.

**Why it works:** Mirrors how decisions actually happen — pain has to be felt before action is taken. The agitate phase is what *makes* the buyer want the solution; without it, you offer relief before they've registered the pain.

**Three examples:**

> *"You spent 6 hours building a client report this week. [P]. And you'll spend 6 hours again next week. And the week after. And by the end of the year that's 312 hours — almost two months of full-time work. [A]. I built a Notion + ChatGPT workflow that does it in 17 minutes. Same quality. Link below. [S]."*

> *"You're posting every day and getting 200 views per post. [P]. Which means after a year of daily posts, you'll still have a tiny audience and you'll be burnt out. [A]. The creators who actually grow stopped posting daily. Here's what they do instead. [S]."*

> *"You signed up for the AI course, the prompts pack, and the agent builder. [P]. You haven't opened any of them. You feel guilty every time you see the notification. The fee renews next month. [A]. Stop buying courses. Buy one workshop where we sit together for 90 minutes and we ship one workflow. [S]."*

**Anti-patterns:**
- Skipping the agitation. "You have a problem. Here's the solution." Too fast.
- Agitating to despair without a credible solution. Emotionally exhausted viewer clicks away.

## Framework 3: BAB (Before-After-Bridge)

Show the current state, paint the desired state, describe the bridge between them. The bridge is your offer.

- **Before:** Where they are now (vivid, painful).
- **After:** Where they could be (specific, believable).
- **Bridge:** Your product as the path between.

**Why it works:** Buyers don't buy products, they buy transformations. BAB explicitly names the transformation, so the buyer can decide if it's worth crossing the bridge.

**Three examples:**

> *"Before: Sunday night dread. Three client reports due Monday. [B]. After: Sunday afternoon, reports already sent, you're outside. [A]. The bridge: a workflow that runs Friday at 5pm and emails the reports to you Sunday morning. [Br]."*

> *"Before: 14 SaaS subscriptions, $890/month, you use 4 of them. [B]. After: 3 tools, $89/month, you use all 3 daily. [A]. The bridge: this audit checklist + the replacement workflows. [Br]."*

> *"Before: scared of ChatGPT, opening it and freezing. [B]. After: a prompt template for every weekly task, 4 minutes per use. [A]. The bridge: this workshop. We build all 12 templates together in one Saturday. [Br]."*

**Anti-patterns:**
- Vague before ("you're struggling") and vague after ("you'll thrive"). Both ends of the bridge must be vivid.
- Bridge that's bigger than the after. If the work to use the product sounds harder than living with the problem, no sale.

## Framework 4: 4Ps (Promise-Picture-Proof-Push)

Fits in static ads, long videos, and email sequences. Heavier on proof than other frameworks.

- **Promise:** What they'll get.
- **Picture:** Paint the vivid version of having it.
- **Proof:** Show it works (testimonial, screenshot, data).
- **Push:** Tell them exactly what to do next.

**Why it works:** The Proof step is the differentiator. When your offer is competing against other similar offers, evidence is what tips the scale.

**Three examples:**

> **Workshop ad:** "Ship your first AI workflow this weekend. [P]. Imagine sending Monday's client work on Sunday and going outside. [P]. Last cohort: 47 of 52 attendees shipped within 7 days. [P]. Saturday at 10am ET. $17. Link below. [P]."

> **Automation product:** "Cut client report time by 80%. [Promise]. Picture: Friday at 4pm, all reports done. You close the laptop. [Picture]. Sarah K., bookkeeper, went from 11 hrs/wk to 90 minutes. [Proof]. Get the template here. [Push]."

> **Course:** "Build your first AI agent in 14 days. [Promise]. Imagine your inbox triaging itself while you sleep. [Picture]. 31 students shipped agents in the last cohort — median time was 9 days. [Proof]. Cohort opens Tuesday. Enroll today, save $200. [Push]."

**Anti-patterns:**
- Promise + Promise + Promise without proof. Reads as hype.
- Picture without promise — "imagine the freedom!" — freedom from what? You skipped the setup.

## Framework 5: AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

The oldest direct-response framework. Still works for emails and long-form content. **Not recommended as default for video ads** in 2026 because it doesn't account for the modern feed environment where you need a *hook* before "attention" can even begin.

- **Attention:** Hook them.
- **Interest:** Build relevance.
- **Desire:** Make them want it.
- **Action:** Tell them what to do.

Use this for email subject lines + bodies, for blog post intros, for landing page copy. Use Hook-Body-CTA, PAS, or BAB for video.

## Framework 6: Extended VSL Structure (3-15 minutes)

For long-form video sales letters that do the work of a sales page. Two flavors:
- **Short VSL (3-6 min):** for warm traffic, lower price points ($17-$297)
- **Long VSL (8-15 min):** for cold traffic, high-ticket ($497+)

**Full structure (Jim Edwards, evolved):**

1. **Pattern Interrupt (0-30 sec):** Shocking statement or visual.
2. **Promise (30-60 sec):** What you're going to share.
3. **Problem (1-2 min):** Pain in vivid detail.
4. **Agitate (2-3 min):** Cost of doing nothing.
5. **Introduce the Solution (3-5 min):** Your product, framed as inevitable.
6. **Proof (5-8 min):** Case studies, testimonials, screenshots, your own results.
7. **The Offer (8-10 min):** What's included, what it costs, bonuses.
8. **Risk Reversal (10-11 min):** Guarantee.
9. **Call to Action (11-12 min):** Specific action.
10. **FAQ / Objection Handling (12-15 min):** "But what about..." section.

For a $17 workshop, you don't need a long VSL. A 60-90 second video on the landing page often outperforms long VSLs at low price points. For $997+, the long VSL pays off because every minute shifts the buyer one step closer.

## Framework 7: Perfect Webinar (Russell Brunson, 45-90 min)

The most documented and battle-tested webinar structure. Used to generate $14M+ in ClickFunnels's first year. Three sections.

### Section 1: Introduction & Origin Story (10-15 min)

Build trust. Establish that you know the problem because you've lived it.

- 0-2 min: Hook, agenda, permission to ask questions
- 2-5 min: Origin story (your version of the audience's current struggle)
- 5-10 min: The turning point (what changed for you)
- 10-15 min: Bridge to "and that's why we're here today"

### Section 2: The Content / Three Secrets (30-45 min)

The core innovation of Perfect Webinar: people don't buy because they have three "false beliefs" stopping them. Your three teaching segments each break one false belief.

For each Secret:
1. State the false belief publicly.
2. Tell a story of someone who believed it and was wrong.
3. Show the new belief.
4. Give one specific tactic that proves the new belief.

The audience leaves each segment with: a corrected belief + a tactic they can use immediately. Three corrections + three tactics = readiness to buy.

### Section 3: The Stack & Close (15-20 min)

The **Stack** is the specific technique for presenting the offer. Build up the value piece by piece before revealing the price:

```
"You're getting:
- The 4-week cohort (value: $1,200)
- 12 done-for-you templates (value: $400)
- Weekly office hours (value: $600)
- Private community access (value: $300)
- The certification credential (value: $200)
Total value: $2,700.
Today's price: $497."
```

Why it works: anchoring. The brain doesn't compute the math; it just registers "value stacked high before price was revealed." $497 sounds small relative to what was just stacked.

Critical: every element must be real. Fake stacking destroys trust.

### Then Q&A (20-30 min extension)

Webinars where attendees average 5-10 reactions each convert at ~69%. Low-engagement webinars convert at <20%. The Q&A is where the conversion actually happens. Don't truncate; extend.

## Framework comparison: when does each framework outperform?

| Situation | Best framework |
|---|---|
| First ad ever, simple offer | Hook-Body-CTA |
| Pain is the primary motivator | PAS |
| Transformation is the primary selling point | BAB |
| You have strong case studies | 4Ps |
| Long sales page or email sequence | AIDA |
| Selling $497+ with cold traffic | Extended VSL |
| Live conversion event | Perfect Webinar |

## Framework + hook combinations that perform best

Frameworks and hooks are independent — any hook can go with any framework. But some combinations have stronger track records:

- **Pain hook + PAS framework** — for cold audience with felt problem
- **Contrarian hook + BAB framework** — for stuck audience needing reframe
- **Specific-Result hook + 4Ps framework** — for warm audience evaluating options
- **Story hook + Hook-Body-CTA** — for almost any audience as a baseline
- **Pattern Interrupt hook + PAS framework** — for cold traffic on visual platforms

When writing three variations, pick three different hook-framework combinations from this list (not three versions of the same combination).
