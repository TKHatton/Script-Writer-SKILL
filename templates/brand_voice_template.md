# Voice & Language Patterns

This is the under-discussed part of script writing. Two writers can use the same framework and one converts at 4x the other because of *how they write the words*. The patterns below are what high-converting copywriters actually do, distilled to a checklist.

## The 10 voice patterns (apply to every script)

### 1. Start mid-thought

Never open with "Hey," "So," "What's up," "Today I want to talk about," "In this video," or "Let me tell you."

These openers signal low-stakes content. The viewer's brain marks the video as skippable and starts scrolling. They're the audio equivalent of a slow intro screen.

**Open mid-thought instead:**
- *"...and that's the third client this month who told me they wasted $5K on a course before finding me."*
- *"Most of us don't have a prompting problem. We have a 'I don't know what to ask' problem."*
- *"I cancelled three SaaS tools last month."*

The "and that's" / "Most of us" / direct-statement openers imply a conversation already in motion. The viewer feels they walked in late, which makes them want to catch up.

### 2. Specific words over abstract words

Every abstract word is a place where the viewer's brain has to fill in meaning. Each fill-in costs you a fraction of attention. Replace:

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "things" | the actual thing |
| "stuff" | the specific item |
| "a lot" | "47" |
| "soon" | "this Saturday" |
| "easy" | "13 minutes" |
| "great results" | "11 hours back per week" |
| "transformation" | the before/after, named |
| "leverage" | "use" |
| "scale" | "go from $5K to $20K/mo" |
| "growth" | the specific growth |
| "amazing" | what's amazing about it |
| "incredible" | the credibility-creating detail |

### 3. Active voice, present tense

Passive voice and future tense both add a distance between the reader and the action. Active + present collapses that distance.

| Passive / future / past | Active / present |
|---|---|
| "It was discovered that..." | "I discovered..." |
| "Results have been achieved" | "Clients are saving 6 hours/week" |
| "You'll be able to..." | "You'll..." |
| "This can help you..." | "This does..." |
| "It is recommended..." | "Do this..." |
| "Mistakes were made..." | "I screwed up..." |

### 4. The "you" ratio

Count every "I" / "we" and every "you" in your script. For most ads, the ratio should be **2:1 or higher in favor of "you."**

The viewer doesn't care about your story except as it illuminates theirs. Your role is to be the mirror, not the subject.

**Exception:** The origin story segment of a webinar, where "I" is the whole point. Even there, the "I" exists to teach the viewer something about their own situation.

### 5. Short sentences, then long ones

Rhythm matters. Three short sentences in a row create urgency. Then one long sentence delivers a meaty point. Then short again.

**Example:**
> "I deleted 11 things. My business doubled. Most people refuse to believe this, but the actual lever isn't doing more — it's doing fewer things with more focus. Here's what I kept."

That's: short, short, long, short. The eye rides the rhythm and the brain doesn't get tired.

### 6. One thought per sentence

Sentences with multiple commas and clauses are where conversion goes to die. If a sentence has more than 20 words, it probably contains two thoughts. Split it.

**Before:**
> "If you're a solopreneur trying to grow your business but you're constantly overwhelmed because you're doing everything yourself and you don't have the time or systems to scale, then this workshop is for you."

**After:**
> "You're doing everything yourself. You don't have the systems. You don't have the time. This workshop fixes that."

### 7. Don't apologize for the pitch

When transitioning to the offer, never say:
- "I hate this part"
- "Okay, salesy time"
- "Sorry to interrupt the value but..."
- "I know this is a lot, but..."

These signals tell the audience the offer is bad. Confidence in the offer is itself part of the offer.

**Instead use:**
- *"If what we covered today resonated, here's what to do next."*
- *"Here's how to keep going."*
- *"For those of you who want to actually do this with me..."*

### 8. The trial close

Russell Brunson's Perfect Webinar has 90+ "trial closes" — moments where the audience implicitly agrees with you. Each is a micro-yes. By the time you ask for the actual yes (buy), you've gotten 30+ smaller yeses. The brain is now in "yes" mode.

**Trial close examples:**
- *"Make sense so far?"*
- *"Anyone else been here?"*
- *"Type 'yes' in the chat if this is you."*
- *"This is the part that flipped it for me — did it click for you?"*
- *"Stay with me on this..."*
- *"Quick check — is this still landing?"*

Use these every 5-10 minutes in long-form, every 30-60 seconds in short-form (more subtle).

### 9. Cut every adverb and most adjectives

Adverbs and adjectives are usually empty calories. They feel descriptive but actually weaken the noun/verb.

**Empty adverbs to cut:** really, very, actually, literally, basically, just, simply, totally, completely, absolutely.

**Before:** "I literally just built this really simple workflow that's actually super effective."
**After:** "I built this workflow. It works."

If a sentence still makes sense without the adverb, delete it.

### 10. Read it aloud

The most common script-writing mistake is writing in "business voice" — the formal register that creeps in when you write down what you'd normally say. Read your draft aloud. Anything that sounds like it belongs in a PowerPoint deck instead of a conversation: rewrite.

**Test:** Would you actually say this sentence to a friend at lunch? If no, rewrite.

## Words and phrases to ban

These signal "generic ad" and trigger the audience's ad-immunity defenses. Avoid all of them:

### Banned hype words
- leverage
- unlock
- synergy
- transformative
- game-changing
- revolutionary
- next-level
- ground-breaking
- cutting-edge
- best-in-class
- world-class
- premium (when not literally premium)
- exclusive (when not literally exclusive)

### Banned hedge words
- might
- could possibly
- some kind of
- a sort of
- in some ways
- perhaps
- maybe
- might be able to

### Banned filler phrases
- "at the end of the day"
- "needless to say"
- "in today's world"
- "in this day and age"
- "the fact of the matter is"
- "let me tell you"
- "I just want to say"

### Banned cliché openers
- "Are you struggling with..."
- "Tired of [generic problem]?"
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Once upon a time..."
- "Let me share with you..."
- "Imagine if you could..."

## Words and phrases that signal credibility

Use these when you have the right context.

### Specificity markers
- "Last Tuesday" (not "recently")
- "$11,847" (not "thousands")
- "47 of 52 students" (not "most students")
- "9 hours and 12 minutes" (not "almost a full workday")
- "by 3pm" (not "fast")

### Vulnerability markers
- "I screwed up"
- "I was wrong about"
- "It cost me $X"
- "I almost quit"
- "I refused to believe..."

### Reframe markers
- "Most people think X. Here's what's actually true."
- "The conventional wisdom says X. The data says Y."
- "I used to believe X. Then..."
- "Wait — that's actually the wrong question."

### Open loops
- "And here's where it gets interesting..."
- "Stay with me, because this is the part that flipped it."
- "I'll tell you what changed in a minute, but first..."
- "There's a thing I haven't told you yet."

## Voice across formats

The same person should sound consistent across formats, but slight tuning helps:

| Format | Voice tuning |
|---|---|
| Short video ad | Urgent, conversational, mid-thought openers |
| Static ad | Punchy, declarative, one sentence per element |
| Carousel | Each slide is its own micro-statement |
| YouTube long-form | Conversational, more permission to build slowly |
| VSL | Persuasive, story-driven, more emotional |
| Webinar | Conversational + trial closes + Q&A engagement |
| Email | Personal, like writing to one friend |

## Brand voice integration

If the user has provided a brand voice document (see `assets/brand_voice_template.md`), apply these layered rules:

1. **Brand voice supersedes default voice** when they conflict (e.g., brand uses formal voice for a luxury offer — match it)
2. **Specificity and "you" ratio still apply** regardless of brand voice
3. **Banned words are still banned** unless the brand explicitly uses one as a trademark
4. **Read-aloud test still applies** — even formal brands should sound like a person, not a press release

If the user hasn't provided brand voice context, default to: direct, specific, conversational, lightly opinionated, no hype.

## Self-check before delivery

Run every script through this voice checklist before showing the user:

- [ ] No "Hey," "So," or "Today" openers
- [ ] No banned hype words (leverage, unlock, synergy, etc.)
- [ ] No banned hedges (might, perhaps, could possibly)
- [ ] "You" appears 2x more than "I/we" (unless origin story)
- [ ] At least one specific number, name, or time reference
- [ ] No adverbs that can be deleted without losing meaning
- [ ] Reads naturally aloud (test it)
- [ ] No sentence longer than 25 words
- [ ] Active voice, present tense
- [ ] No apology for the pitch

If any check fails, rewrite before delivering.
